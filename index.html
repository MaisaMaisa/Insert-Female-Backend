[ Insert Female Backend ]

Dec 14, 2024 – Feb 22, 2025

Opening: Saturday, Dec 14, 6-9pm

Panel Discussion, moderated by Tara McPherson: Sunday, Dec 15, 2-4pm

The most popular tools for building a website require zero to minimal coding knowledge. Dragged and dropped content in the colorful back-ends is published on the website's front-end in no time. All you have to do is click Publish. This web design automation puts us far from questioning the software logic and the rules of the language these tools root from, let alone study the art of programming. The last 30 years of Cyberfeminism can teach us that vibrant cultures and certain political situations can be read through the interfaces that shaped today's internet. The most enlightening era of the 1990s was when the first online collectives were formed, hypertext and literature were merged, default binaries within the programming languages were questioned, and wild personal interfaces ("online graffiti art") were massively self-published. In the early 2000s, however, social media platforms pushed these examples to the bottom of the search results, further fading them out of our user imagination and memory. Proposals for alternative internet were no longer present in the daily user discussion. An average user became an average consumer of information, bits, and bodies. Today, the mainstream arithmetic logics behind the popular tools and technologies are being picked up by AI in rapid speed aka maintaining and making the mainstream way more accessible than it ever was. The cost of extreme user-friendliness is user unhappiness.

JOAN presents [ Insert Female Backend ] — a collective exhibition in which other-than-arithmetic web logics, methods, and aesthetics for the internet are deployed as proposals for a new digital future. Molly Soda scatters more than two decades worth of neglected digital content in no particular order to question the rules of institutional archiving traceable in the digital. Maya Man develops a Javascript script that generates non-sensical inspirational quotes for women as a method for blurring the need for authenticity in the performance of online self. Chia Amisola performs the girl-saint to question the theologies integrated in the machines. At the intersection of art and design, Clara Pasteau builds a writing tool that enables a meditative writing experience in the context of a loud internet. To urgently address the need for solidarity with people of Palestine, Noam Youngrak Son develops three tools that turn the user into an activist. As a literary experiment, Theo Ellin Ballew re-launches Oral Pub — an online publication for interactive literature.

Detailed descriptions of the artworks included in the exhibition:

𝙢𝙤𝙡𝙡𝙮𝙨𝙨𝙩𝙪𝙛𝙛 (by Molly Soda) pulls from over two decades worth of content accumulated by the artist Molly Soda. 2000 files were lovingly hand-selected from her assorted hard drives, computers, and online accounts and distributed amongst 20 folders—like dropping party favors into little bags. Each folder houses 100 files: .jpegs, .gifs, .pdfs, .movs, .pngs, .mp3s + more.

The.zip folder is physically embodied as a 𝙪𝙣𝙞𝙦𝙪𝙚 artist publication. Files are printed and assembled to populate a 3-ring binder. Videos are broken down into frames. Old webcam portraits become 4x6 keepsakes. Glitter graphics are printed on transparency paper. Spammy emails and Instagram ads litter the pages. Brought together in an artist's book, the files overlap and complicate each other, giving weight to often neglected digital content. Each binder comes with a signed certificate of authenticity.

FAKE IT TILL YOU MAKE IT: https://fakeittillyoumakeit.lol/(by Maya Man) borrows from the bubbly language and pastel aesthetics of text driven instagram graphics to scrutinize the promotion of wellness, self-care, and confidence on social media.

Online, these types of posts survive on likes, comments, and shares. What do I believe? becomes What do I want to appear to believe? Fake it till you make it! Maybe your dream life lives here: In a digital, fantasy world where the algorithm plays god and loving yourself feels like looking into the light of your screen.

Computer Dream Angel Girl Saint: https://i-love-everything.com/world/cathedral/ (by Chia Amisola) -- a performance and a lecture tracing the artist's Catholic faith, Marian apparitions, feminine technologies, surveillance & delusion, Ishmael Bernal’s Himala, and the nature of faith, truth, and presence.

In an age where gods are indecipherable from machines, all bodies are heavens, technology is devotion, and delusions are divine. Weaving through the infrastructural role of technologies & relics, the feminine maternal roles of computers as containers, and the surveillant, performative nature of the girl-saint is a performance-lecture on gods, girls, gazing, and glitches.

The presentation previews Himala, a WIP durational desktop performance piece by Amisola, reenacting the mysteries of the rosary atop of the operating system & browser. An imagination and ritual of virginal software as a digital counterpoint to the Philippine Pasyón from the perspective of the girl-saint: the pure, sacred, transitory, and surveilled object of the divine.

Tranquil Words Today: https://tranquil-words.today/ (by Clara Pasteau) is a writing tool. It aims to be helpful when having a writing practice or wanting to experiment with automatic writing, surrounded by a loud internet.

    How it works:
  • Start writing
  • You won’t be able to see your text, it’s i……n…v…….i..…s….i.…b…l……e
  • Let go…
  • And keep writing
  • Stay here
  • Don’t change tab, or you will lose your text!
  • Click on the clock if you want a 10’ timer
  • You can change the background color depending on your mood
    Tech insights:
  • Built with Html, Css and Javascript only
  • There is no database
  • You can’t right click
  • You can’t select your text
  • You can’t delete your text
  • If you change window, your text will vanish and you will have to start again

Forbidden Colors Translator: https://www.d-act.org/forbiddencolors.html, 1.8 Million Displaced Here: https://www.d-act.org/rafah.html, and From the River to the Sea: https://www.d-act.org/riversea.html(by Noam Youngrak Son) are 3 x web tools for solidarity with people of Palestine.

ORAL.pub: https://oral.pub/ (by Theo Ellin Ballew) publishes poetry/art made for and inseparable from the web. ORAL.pub sites take advantage of their medium; their modes of interaction are meaningful and often defy our internet instincts. Many ORAL.pub sites are translated or multilingual, and all are brought into physical sites for corpORAL parties.

Tech Imposters — a platform that collects contemporary experiences of web artists, designers, and wizards and which is made by the curator Maisa Imamović, will be set up in the exhibition space to collect the stories of JOAN’s publics.

This exhibition is part of JOAN’s second Chapter of programming, which runs from April 2024 through July 2025, and brings together artists and collectives that explore varying communal structures of intervention and support, as experiments towards liberatory futures.

Amalia Soto, known as Molly Soda, is a Brooklyn-based internet performance artist. Soda works across a variety of digital platforms, producing selfies videos, GIFs, zines, and web-based performance art, which are presented both online and in gallery installations in a variety of forms. Molly Soda's work explores the technological mediation of self-concept, contemporary feminism, cyberfeminism, mass media and popular social media culture. Molly Soda is the co-editor with Arvida Byström of the 2017 book Pics or It Didn't Happen: Images Banned from Instagram.

Maya Man is an artist focused on contemporary identity culture on the internet. Her websites, generative series, and installations examine dominant narratives around femininity, authenticity, and the performance of self online. She is the creator of the browser extension Glance Back and the Art Blocks curated collection FAKE IT TILL YOU MAKE IT. She has exhibited internationally at bitforms, NYC; SOOT, Tokyo; Verse, London; HEK, Basel; and Feral File, online. She has been invited to speak on her work at The New Museum, NYC; The V&A, London; and MOCA, Los Angeles. Her artwork has been featured in Art in America, Forbes, Vogue, Dazed, and more. She is online at mayaontheinter.net.

Chia Amisola (b. 2000, Manila) is an internet & ambient artist devoted to the internet’s loss, love, labor, and liberation. Through websites, tools, research, gathering, and performance, their (web)site-specific art imagines creation as synonymous with liberation. Their 'internet ambient' explores the internet's ecologies, territory, infrastructure, and intimacies. ( Mostly, they want to gather all the people they love in one place, and build an internet that could be that place. )

They steward Developh (a community of critical practice for Filipino technologists, established in 2016) and the Philippine Internet Archive (nurturing publications & interventions preserving network cultures).

Clara Pasteau is a graphic designer, web developer and a newly member of OSP (Open Source Publishing) in Brussels. She is particularly interested in considering coding as a craftspersonship, thinking of HTML and CSS as raw building materials. Her artistic and research practice revolves around websites as a medium of analysis, with inquiries that encompass technological, political, and ecological aspects. She also uses writing as a starting point in my projects, intuitively and automatically.

Noam Youngrak Son is a communication designer practicing queer publishing. They explore revolutionary methods of disseminating deviant narratives. In their work, publishing concerns more than mere printed matter, since, in its etymology, the word "publishing" originally meant to populate communities and to breed interspecies relations. The term "queer" in this context is not used as a statement of identity but as that of methodology – small yet collective strategies of publishing that challenge the modern myth of a singular, heroic “Genius” designer.

Theo Ellin Ballew (deepest known matronym Srebrna or Silver) writes poetry and/or code. Their first book of poems, I'm from the Desert, is a cyberpunk life story forthcoming with Ornithopter Press--and their static writing also appears in Everybody Press Journal; 1080 Press's 240P; Works & Days; Action, Spectacle;3AM; Juked; Litro; Bone Bouquet; and elsewhere. Their mobile writing (or browser-based art) has been or will soon be shown with Ghost Proposal; Compás 88; External Pages; Crawlspace.cool; The Wrong Biennale; Tagwverk; screen_; El Quinto Piso; Electric Literature Organization; Bandini Espacio Cultural;IK-Projects; and Yeah Maybe. Theo also founded and directs ORAL.pub, a literary journal of web-native poetry/art. Theo has taught computational art/poetry and cyberfeminism in departments like RISD's Digital + Media, NYU's Interactive Telecommunications Program, Brown's Literary Arts, Brooklyn College's Art Department, and The New School's Communication Design. They hold an MEA in Literary Arts Digital Language Arts) from Brown and a BA in Literature trom Yale. Theo grew up in the greatest Southwest and on the internet.

Tara McPherson is the The HMH Foundation Endowed Chair for the Study of Censorship in Media and Professor of Cinema and Media Studies at the University of Southern California’s School of Cinematic Arts and Director of the Sidney Harman Academy for Polymathic Studies.  She is a core Ph.D. advisor in the MAP program, USC’s innovative practice based-Ph.D., and also an affiliated faculty member in the American Studies and Ethnicity Department. Her research engages the cultural dimensions of media, including the intersection of gender, race, affect and place.  She has a particular interest in digital media.  Her research focuses on the digital humanities, early software histories, gender, and race, as well as upon the development of new tools and paradigms for digital publishing, learning, and authorship. She is currently examining the spread of hate through online networks and making anti-fascist media through The Reclaim Project.
